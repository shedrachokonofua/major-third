I have been waiting my whole life for an album like this
This is already my favourite album of the last few years. In fact I'd have to go all the way back to 2012 and Fiona Apple's Idler Wheel to find an album I like more. With every listen, I come away with even more appreciation for it.

On Titanic Rising, Weyes Blood draws from many distinct influences, and even though she reaches into the past and tugs on our heartstrings with nostalgia, the music sounds so fresh. I've never heard anything like this before, and yet it immediately feels so familiar. Every sound feels like it belongs, and every song feels necessary. Theres a rich blend of genres at play, and on paper, you'd think that some of them wouldn't work well together, but they shine. For example, a few songs combine Chamber and Baroque Pop with a Country sound, resulting in dreamy earworms that you just have to sing along to. Its early days yet (the album hasn't even been officially released, for God's sake), but I think the hype is well-deserved, and predict that in time, this album will be seen as one of the essential Art Pop albums.

intoatticdust Mar 21 2019
+5 Vote downBookmarkVote up
Weyes Blood loves the movies. She's very upfront about this fact - on the track Movies, she sings "I know the meaning / I know the story / I know the glory / I love movies…" And her latest album, Titanic Rising, takes its name from the long-maligned sequel to Titanic, in and of itself a winking bit of movie lore. Even without the context of Movies and the title of the piece as a whole, it's clear her love is genuine - because Titanic Rising (the album) is an album thoroughly fascinated with the movies.

On a visceral level, Weyes Blood's instrumentation throughout the album seems to evoke movie soundtracks of yore, trading in the piano and horns of Front Row Seat to Earth for a full string section and synthesizers used heavily throughout. Not a song on the album is without strings - even the most experimental "electronic" track in Mirror Forever has some creep in at the end, for great effect. This is an unusual choice in late 10s pop music, but Mering's eye for arrangements makes it a powerful tool - whether in the soaring opener A Lot's Gonna Change or the swerve Movies takes as the piece suddenly opens up, she makes masterful use of the added depth.

But what remains from her last album is, of course, her incredible vocal delivery. With her level of mastery, it becomes another instrument that slots seamlessly into the rest of her ensemble, becoming one voice or many harmonizing notes at a moment's notice. Andromeda, the lead single from the album, exploits this for maximum effect as she reaches into her upper register against the background of a synthesizer that feels pulled straight from an 80s film, harmonizing with herself and strings which become indistinguishable from the artificial synths.

What really makes this album work, what makes it truly essential, is the clarity of purpose throughout. Even the lyrics are fascinated with movies - and the deeper implications of an obsession with fantasy and escaping. Everyday (a song which I could write a whole essay about by itself) pines for genuine human connection in a world where the genuine is becoming increasingly rare. Something To Believe simply begs the world for any sign at all - "Give me something I can see / Something bigger and louder than the voices in me / Something to believe..."

Where the A-side leans into more analog instrumentation, the B-side toys with more experimental synthesizers and song structures, including the aforementioned Movies and stellar Mirror Forever, where Weyes Blood plays the role of jilted lover to a backdrop of snaps and drums which spin around and envelop you while vague impressions of violins and flutes drift in and out of focus, all anchored to more precise and awe-inspiring vocal work.

The album is perhaps best represented in miniature with the last two tracks. On pseudo-closer Picture Me Better, a relatively brief piece, Weyes Blood reiterates all the previous themes of the album - missed connections and retreat into fantasy - with a quiet and mournful love song, where she begs for "something with meaning to come through." And in the final instrumental track, Nearer To Thee, the previously muddled and distorted opening half-minute of the album is rendered again with only strings, calling back to that initial hopeful note as we watch the lights in the theater go up, and the end credits begin to roll.
